Obq_Fresnel2Standard_Compounds

Category: Compounds

Output: Values for the Arnold Standard Shader.

These compounds are used along the Obq_Fresnel shader and the Arnold Standard Shader in order to have energy conserving colored scales. They differ in the type of material they represent.
Documentation

Before use Make sure all scale values of the Standard shader are set to 0 and that all fresnel features are turned off.
How to connect Simply connect an Obq_Fresnel in each Fresnel Ports of a compound.
  • Fresnel Metal inputs are for use with the "Metal " Material Type of the Obq_Fresnel.
  • Fresnel Refractive inputs are for use with the "Refractive " Material Type of the Obq_Fresnel.
You must also connect your texture nodes in the appropriate inputs. Connect the outputs of the compound to the corresponding inputs of the Standard.

Don't forget to change the IOR of the standard according to the, lets say, green value of the IOR of the Obq_Fresnel for refractive material types.
General idea The priority of energy consumption of parameters is in a top to bottom order in the compound gui. In other words, if visually the Ks is above the Kd, only what is not consumed by the specular scale will be passed down to the diffuse scale. Note that the scales in the compounds does not represent absolute values like the ones in the Standard, the represent the ratio of the available energy. Krs means Kr or Ks.

Exemple : In priority order : Ks above Kd.
  • Ks = 1 and Kd = 1 : Ks will take 100% of the energy leaving 0% to the Kd.
  • Ks = 0.5 and Kd = 0.5 : Ks will take 50% of the energy and Kd will take 50% of (100%-50%) = 25%.
This means that the compound will never create more energy, but it can absorb some. If you want to be sure energy is conserved, all scales should be 1 or at least the diffuse scale (Kd) if it exists.

Another Example ( Obq_Fresnels2Standard_Dielectric ): Fresnel outputs a color < (1,1,1) that is a colored reflection scale. The Kr multiplies that value. (1,1,1) minus the result will yield the light not reflected. The Kt then multiplies that value to give the transmitted light. The light that is passed down to specular and diffuse is the difference between the light not reflected and the transmitted light, meaning that its the light that wasn't reflected nor refracted. Unless you had fun with the Kt ( Kt < 1 ), no light will be passed down, otherwise, it will be split according to the priority of the scales like in the previous example.
Compounds Here is a list of the different compounds and their uses :
  • Obq_Fresnel2Standard_SimpleMetal: This is probably the simplest compounds and is for metals that might have a diffuse component.
  • Obq_Fresnel2Standard_PerforatedMetal: This is a strange compounds that is used for this metal plates perforated by tiny holes. You must uncheck "Opaque" in Arnold properties for this to work. To better approximate this, there's a checkbox to enable opacity based on incidence ray/normal.
  • Obq_Fresnel2Standard_LayeredDiffuse: The "Layered" series are a nice way to simulate a material covered with a refracting coating like varnish for example. Note that the Kt_color tint the transmitted light. This one only allows the layer underneath the varnish to be diffuse.
  • Obq_Fresnel2Standard_LayeredNonMetal: Is a generalization of the previous one by adding a specular term to the layer underneath.
  • Obq_Fresnel2Standard_LayeredMetal : Another "Layered" version but this time, 2 Obq_Fresnel are required: one refractive type for the varnish and one metal type for the layer underneath.
  • Obq_Fresnel2Standard_SimpleDielectric : The simplest compound for dielectrics like water, glass, gems, etc.  
  • Obq_Fresnel2Standard_Dielectric : A generalization of the previous compound. Note that this time, the Kt_color doesn't tint the the specular nor the diffuse part because the rays didn't pass through the refracted part before.
SSS But what about SSS ? Well, this one you'll have to set it on your own in the Standard because it doesn't depend on the fresnel term at the shading point. If you don't want to add too much energy with it, I would suggest to lower the scales to account for scattered light.
